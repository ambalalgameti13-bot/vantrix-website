Trump Pivots to Fox News Doctor for Surgeon General After Tensions Sink First Pick

May 03, 2026 · Vantrix Global Team

A Sudden Shift in the Surgeon General Nomination

President Trump made a dramatic about-face in his quest to fill the nation's top medical post, withdrawing the nomination of lifestyle influencer Casey Means and replacing her with Dr. Nicole B. Saphier, a breast radiologist and Fox News contributor. The announcement, delivered in a series of blistering social media posts on Thursday, signals a volatile nomination process that reflects broader ideological battles within the Republican party.

The Fallout Over Casey Means

Means, a Stanford-trained physician who left clinical practice to build a lucrative wellness empire, became a flashpoint for controversy after her February confirmation hearing. Her nomination stalled in the Senate Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions Committee, chaired by Sen. Bill Cassidy (R-La.), who voiced concerns over her vaccine skepticism and lack of an active medical license. Without a license, Means would have been the first surgeon general in decades unable to practice medicine—a point her critics seized upon.

During the hearing, Means deflected questions about vaccines, avoiding direct endorsements of immunization or alignment with anti-vaccine Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. This ambiguity, coupled with her history of promoting unproven supplements and diagnostic tests, eroded support among key Republicans. Her book, co-authored with her brother, includes a chapter titled "Trust Yourself, Not Your Doctor," which further alienated medical professionals.

Trump's response was characteristically blunt. In a post, he called Cassidy "a very disloyal person" who "stood in the way of Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s Nominee." He then thanked Means, declaring she would continue fighting for the Make America Health Again (MAHA) movement, and urged Louisiana voters to oust Cassidy in the next election.

Enter Dr. Nicole Saphier

Within 15 minutes of that broadside, Trump announced Saphier as his new nominee, praising her as a "STAR physician" and "INCREDIBLE COMMUNICATOR." Saphier, a breast radiologist at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, also founded an herbal supplement company and has publicly questioned vaccine mandates. Her medical degree comes from Ross University School of Medicine in Barbados, and she has been a regular Fox News contributor, often advocating for patient autonomy in medical decisions.

The rapid pivot underscores the administration's preference for media-savvy, ideologically aligned doctors over traditional medical establishment figures. Saphier's nomination, however, is likely to face similar scrutiny over her vaccine views and supplement business ties, raising questions about whether the Senate will approve her.

This episode illustrates the growing influence of the MAHA movement and the administration's willingness to bypass conventional political norms to install health leaders who challenge mainstream medicine. For now, the surgeon general's office remains vacant, its future occupant a symbol of the ongoing culture war over public health.
